Inspired by Apple Aqua

Apple Aqua

Jobs said you'd want to lick it.
We prove that beauty and usability aren't opposites.

Aqua proved that software could be beautiful. When Jobs unveiled those wet, translucent buttons at Macworld 2000, he demonstrated that an interface could provoke a physical, emotional response — delight, desire, trust. People wanted to press those buttons because they looked delicious. That was the strength: emotional impact as a design tool.

The weakness? The textures fought the content. Pinstripes competed with text. Brushed metal clashed with brushed metal. Every surface screamed for attention and nothing felt quiet. This page keeps the emotional conviction — the gel highlights, the traffic lights, the tactile depth — and strips away the visual noise. The result is an interface that still makes you want to lick it, but also lets you get your work done.

Principle 01

The Gel Button

The signature move. A rounded pill shape with a three-stop vertical gradient and a white-to-transparent highlight on the top half. That highlight is the whole trick — it simulates light hitting a convex, glossy surface. Like a gumdrop. Like a jellybean. Like something your brain says "this is pressable" before your hand moves. Click them. They want to be clicked.

/* The Aqua gel button — the lickable gradient */
border-radius: 20px;
background: linear-gradient(180deg,
  #6EC6F5 0%,    /* light top — sky reflection */
  #2F80ED 50%,   /* body color — the candy */
  #1A6AD4 100%   /* dark bottom — shadow */
);

/* The glossy highlight — ::before pseudo on top half */
background: linear-gradient(180deg,
  rgba(255,255,255,0.55) 0%,  /* bright top */
  rgba(255,255,255,0.25) 40%, /* fade */
  rgba(255,255,255,0.05) 100% /* nearly gone */
);
Principle 02

Brushed Metal & Pinstripes

Two textures that defined a decade. Pinstripes — subtle vertical lines at 2px intervals — covered every window background in early OS X. They whispered "this is a content area." Then came brushed metal for the "pro" apps: iTunes, QuickTime, Finder. Horizontal noise bands that said "this is chrome, this is the frame." Both built with nothing but repeating-linear-gradient.

/* Pinstripes — the Aqua wallpaper */

  0deg,
  transparent, transparent 1px,
  rgba(0,0,0,0.03) 1px,
  rgba(0,0,0,0.03) 2px
);

/* Brushed metal — horizontal banding via many stops */
background: linear-gradient(180deg,
  #d4d8dc 0%, #c8ccd2 4%, #d0d4d8 8%,
  #c4c8ce 12%, #ccd0d6 16% ...
); /* 26 stops = convincing noise */
Principle 03

The Traffic Light Controls

Red, yellow, green. Close, minimize, zoom. The most recognizable UI element in computing history. Three 12-pixel circles with gel gradients and a tiny white reflection dot. On hover, they reveal their purpose: ×, –, +. Apple nailed the principle: consistent, discoverable, and instantly memorable. Twenty-five years later, they haven't changed the metaphor. They refined the rendering.

What This Is Not

  • ✗ Gratuitous glossiness on every surface
  • ✗ Textures that slow down rendering
  • ✗ Mixing Aqua and Brushed Metal without purpose (Apple's own mistake)
  • ✗ Photorealistic UI elements that fight the content
  • ✓ Gel highlights as clear affordance — "this is pressable"
  • ✓ Texture to distinguish surface types (chrome vs content)
  • ✓ Traffic light pattern — consistent, memorable window controls
  • ✓ The radical idea that software should be beautiful

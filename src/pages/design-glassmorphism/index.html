Inspired by Glassmorphism

Glassmorphism

Apple frosted the glass again.
We're asking what's behind it.

2020. Apple launches macOS Big Sur and suddenly every designer on Dribbble remembers that translucency exists. Rounder corners, gradient backgrounds, frosted panels everywhere. It was Aero's ghost, dressed in San Francisco font and a pastel palette.

The community ran with it. Everything became frosted glass overnight. But underneath the hype? A genuinely smart idea about context through transparency — letting background content bleed through your UI to create spatial awareness. That's the part worth keeping.

Principle 01

Frosted Glass

backdrop-filter: blur() with a semi-transparent background. That's the entire trick. The background bleeds through, softened into an ambient glow. Too little blur and you get noise. Too much and you've painted a wall and called it glass. The sweet spot is where you can sense what's behind the panel without being able to read it.

Principle 02

The Border Trick

This is the detail that separates "I tried glassmorphism" from "I understand glassmorphism." A subtle 1px border with low-opacity white. That's it. It creates the glass edge — the refraction line where light catches the rim of a real glass panel. Without it, your frosted panel just floats there, undefined, like a blurry box someone forgot to finish.

WITH BORDER
Defined
1px rgba(255,255,255,0.25)
WITHOUT
Lost
Same glass, no edge
/* The border that makes or breaks it */
border: 1px solid rgba(255, 255, 255, 0.25);

/* Bonus: inset highlight on top edge simulates light */
box-shadow: inset 0 1px 0 rgba(255, 255, 255, 0.2);
Principle 03

Layered Depth

Multiple glass panels at different opacities create spatial hierarchy without drop shadows. The further back, the more transparent. The further forward, the more frosted. Your eye reads it as depth automatically — same principle as atmospheric perspective in painting. But here's the trap: stack three glass layers and your phone's GPU starts sweating. Two layers max. That's the rule.

Layer 1 — 8% opacity, background context
Layer 2 — 14% opacity, content group
Layer 3 — overkill (but pretty)
Two layers create hierarchy. Three layers create a performance review.
All Together

Put It Together

Frosted glass + the border trick + layered depth. Three principles, one dashboard. The gradient background bleeds through meaningfully. The borders define each panel. Nothing is stacked three deep.

What This Is Not

  • ✗ Glass over meaningless gradient blobs
  • ✗ Stacking glass on glass on glass
  • ✗ Frosted panels with no visible content behind them
  • ✗ "It looks like macOS" as the design goal
  • ✓ Glass that reveals underlying content
  • ✓ The 1px white border — small but essential
  • ✓ One glass layer, maybe two. Never three
  • ✓ Test on low-end devices. backdrop-filter is expensive

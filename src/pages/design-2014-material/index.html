Inspired by Android Lollipop

Material

Paper, ink, and physics made a billion phones feel designed.
We keep the spatial logic. Free it from the template.

Material Design gave motion meaning. The ripple tells you "I heard you, right here." The shadow tells you "I'm above that." The easing curve tells you "I came from there." No design system before had treated physics as a language.

The problem was rigidity. Every app became a Google app. The spec was so prescriptive — "cards at 2dp, dialogs at 24dp, FAB at bottom-right" — that it became a template, not a toolkit.

We keep the physics. We drop the dogma. Elevation as hierarchy, ripple as honest feedback, bold color as identity — these are principles worth preserving. The 300-page spec telling you exactly where to put the FAB is not.

Principle 01

Paper Elevation

Every surface is a sheet of paper at a specific height. The shadow isn't a style choice — it's a z-coordinate. Google specified exact dp values for every component. We don't follow the table religiously, but we keep the rule: higher means more important. Hover these cards — they rise, and their shadow tells you they did.

Principle 02

Ink Ripple

The most honest piece of touch feedback ever designed. A ripple radiates from the exact point of contact — not a generic highlight, not a color swap. A physics-based wave that confirms "yes, here, I felt that." On light surfaces the ink is dark. On colored surfaces it's light. We keep this. Every interactive surface on this page ripples from where you actually clicked.

Principle 03

Meaningful Motion

Nothing teleports. Elements expand from their origin, transform into their destination. The easing curve is cubic-bezier(0.4, 0, 0.2, 1) — fast in, slow out. Google called it "standard easing." It answers "where did this come from?" If the animation can't answer that question, kill it. Click a chip to see it expand from where it was.

Bold Color

Material shipped a palette of named colors at fixed saturation stops — 500 for primary, 700 for the status bar, A200 for accents. The color band at the top of every app was the app's identity. We borrow the confidence: pick a bold primary, commit to it, and let it carry the interface. No pastels. No hedging.

Components

Raised, Flat, FAB

Three button types, each with a clear role. Raised buttons cast shadow — they're for primary actions that need to stand out. Flat buttons sit flush — they're for secondary actions inside cards or dialogs. The FAB floats above everything — one per screen, the single most important action. We keep this hierarchy. The uppercase is Lollipop's voice. The ripple on click is its handshake.

All Together

Lollipop Inbox

Status bar at 700. App bar at 500. Cards with 2dp shadow. A FAB hovering in the corner. Ripple on every touch. The original Material formula — paper, ink, bold color, physics — assembled into the app that defined an era. Every Android app looked like this from 2014 to 2021. We show it here to honor the system, not to copy it.

What This Is Not

  • ✗ The 300-page spec — we take principles, not prescriptions
  • ✗ Every app looking like Gmail — your app has its own identity
  • ✗ FAB on every screen — not everything needs a primary action
  • ✗ Ripple on static text — ink responds to interaction only
  • ✓ Elevation as spatial hierarchy — higher means louder
  • ✓ Ink ripple at the exact point of touch — honest feedback
  • ✓ Bold primary color as identity — pick one, commit
  • ✓ Motion that explains origin — no teleporting, no mystery

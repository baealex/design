Inspired by Neumorphism

Neumorphism

Dribbble dreamed in monochrome.
The shadows were gorgeous. The usability wasn't.

It was 2019. Designers were bored — flat design felt sterile, Material's drop shadows felt formulaic. Then someone on Dribbble asked: what if the entire interface was one material, and elements emerged from it like clay?

They called it "New Skeuomorphism" — later shortened to Neumorphism. Soft extruded surfaces. Two shadows doing all the work. No borders, no gradients, no color. It went viral overnight. Every calculator app and music player on the internet got the treatment.

The technique? Genuinely clever. The result in production? An accessibility disaster. We're keeping the clever part.

Principle 01

The Double Shadow

One dark shadow cast to the bottom-right. One light shadow cast to the top-left. That's the entire trick. The dark shadow mimics an absence of light. The light shadow mimics a reflection. Together: box-shadow creates the illusion of physical extrusion. It's the only CSS technique that makes a surface look like it's pushing out of the page — not floating above it, but growing from it.

Principle 02

Pressed vs Raised

Invert the shadows with inset and the surface flips from extruded to pressed. That's two states from one technique. Click the elements below — raised becomes inset, extruded becomes carved. The duality is elegant: you only need one pair of shadow colors to describe an entire interaction model. Push in, pop out. Nothing else needed.

Principle 03

Monochrome Discipline

Here's the constraint that makes Neumorphism Neumorphism: everything is the same base color. Background, foreground, buttons, cards — all #e0e5ec. Shadows do all the work. No borders. No gradients. No accent colors. The discipline is beautiful. It's also why the whole thing collapses in production — when everything is the same color, nothing has affordance. But as a creative constraint? It breeds remarkable focus.

Three values. That's the entire color system. One background, one dark shadow, one light shadow. Every surface on this page uses only these three. The visual complexity comes entirely from how light interacts with depth.

All Together

Put It Together

Double shadows + pressed/raised states + monochrome constraint. Three principles, one mini player. Everything is the same gray. Shadows carve out the interface. It looks like a Dribbble shot from 2020 — because it is. But it works.

What This Is Not

  • ✗ Neumorphic primary buttons — invisible affordance
  • ✗ Text on neumorphic surfaces without contrast
  • ✗ Navigation using only neumorphism — where do I click?
  • ✗ "Looks good on Dribbble" as a design rationale
  • ✓ Double shadows for decorative depth
  • ✓ Pressed state as clear interaction feedback
  • ✓ Monochrome constraint breeds creativity
  • ✓ Supplement with contrast — don't rely on shadows alone

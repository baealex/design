October 25, 2001. Windows XP launched with Luna — a visual style that threw away every gray, boxy convention of the 90s. Suddenly, titlebars were blue. The Start button was green. Buttons had gradient highlights. The default wallpaper was a photograph of a green hill under a perfect sky called "Bliss", and it became the most viewed image in human history.

Luna wasn't subtle. It was aggressively friendly. Every pixel said: don't be afraid of this machine. It's sunny outside. Everything is fine. Click the green button.

The design world mocked it. Users loved it. And two decades later, that blue-and-green warmth still hits different.

Principle 01

The Glossy Blue Button

This is the icon of the era. A rounded blue button with a glossy highlight splitting it horizontally — bright on top, deeper below. It looks like a drop of sky captured in gel. The Koreans have a perfect word for it: tangtanghada (탱글탱글) — something soft but taut, like pressing on a rubber ball that pushes back. That bounce, that friendly resistance — that is the Luna button.

Principle 02

Nature as Interface

The background is the design. Blue sky at the top, green earth at the bottom. Not a photograph — a gradient. An emotional gradient that says: it is a clear day, everything is fine, your computer is your friend. Windows XP shipped with "Bliss" — a photo of a green hill under a blue sky — and it became the most viewed image in human history. That was not an accident. That was design philosophy.

Principle 03

The Friendly Surface

Every panel in Luna says the same thing: welcome. White backgrounds, rounded corners, gentle shadows. No sharp edges, no dark themes, no brutalist aggression. These surfaces feel like a handshake — open, warm, non-threatening. The rounded corners signal that nothing here will hurt you. The soft shadows say: this floats. It is light. It is easy.

A Sunny Day Widget

Nature gradient + glossy buttons + friendly white surfaces. Three principles, one interface. A weather widget that feels like opening a window. This is what Luna believed: your screen should feel like looking outside on the best day of the year.

What This Is Not

⚠
Design Guidelines
  • ✗ Stock photography of happy people sitting on grass
  • ✗ Gradients without purpose or emotional direction
  • ✗ Corporate wallpaper masquerading as design
  • ✗ Glossy everything — some surfaces should breathe
  • ✓ Nature gradients as emotional backdrop — "technology serves life"
  • ✓ Glossy buttons that feel tactile and responsive
  • ✓ Warm, optimistic color temperature throughout
  • ✓ The radical belief that computers should make you smile

It wasn't just an OS.
It was the first computer that smiled at you.

Luna made a radical bet: that a computer could feel warm, safe, and inviting. That blue titlebar, that green Start button, that impossible hill under a perfect sky — they weren't decoration. They were a handshake.
Every pixel said the same thing: welcome home.
